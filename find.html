Flooding in Edinburgh

Water of Leith

The Water of Leith flows through the North West Pentlands, through the city, until it joins the Forth Estuary at the port of Leith. Over the past 200 years the river has been heavily modified through dams, weirs, lochs, and culverts to accommodate the economy and continuous urban development. But this has led to flood vulnerability in 73 km2 of its 122 km2 catchment.

After the April 2000 flood, the city of Edinburgh implemented the Flood Risk Management Act and flood prevention schemes, for which walls, gates and other flood defenceswere built. The Harlaw, Threipmuir and Harperring reservoirs were also purchased to improve management.

Learn about the history of the Murray Burn and Water of Leith in the Gazetteer for Scotland.

Water of Leith Catchment

Flooding

Due to a combination of coastal, surface and river flooding, SEPA declared 85% of the city of Edinburgh as “in need of protection”, and stated that 284,000 homes, businesses and services were at risk of flooding in 2018, with an increment of 110,000 by 2080s, which could cost the government £31.5 million per year. Factors such as an inadequate drainage systems, poor building construction techniques, and outdated flood defences, combined with homes and businesses already located in flood zones have amplified the risk of flooding.

Pressures and Opportunities

the catchment is of poor ecological status due to ten impassable barriers, the presence of invasive species, and sewage and farming runoff along the Murray Burn, especially in a culvert. Despite the continuous riparian woodland along the main stream, increased use of the Water of Leith Walk and new developments are causing fragmentation and deterioration of the corridors quality.
The Water of Leith management plan was set to address some of these issues but only focuses on the popular Water of Leith Walk. The natural flow of this section was mostly maintained due to its hard rock river bed and has seen an increase in conservation efforts through numerous action plans. The Murray Burn on the other hand faces higher pressures with pollution and the culvert but has mostly been neglected by action plans and studies due to its smaller size.

Traditional river management have been effective defences against small flooding, but as seen in 2000 were not effective enough for bigger floods. They currently restrict the natural flow of the river and add additional pressure onto the system. The design of the culvert can easily lead to blockages due to debris, increasing the likelihood of flooding in the area. The sewage system runs parallel to the culvert and its overflow is not restricted during flooding events.
New urban developments along the river were observed, which will not only be directly impacted by flooding, but also create more impermeable surfaces reducing water infiltration and increasing surface runoff.

Based on SEPA Flood, Population at Risk and River Management data: Murray Burn, Saughton Park, Murrayfield and Inverleith Parkwere considered flood management opportunities. Following the natural river management guidelines, solutions such as offline storage areas, leaky barriers, creating floodplain and floodplain woodlands are to be considered. The efficiency of these methods depends on the number of storage areas and the total storage volume. Considering the large amount of storage needed, Saughton Park only has limited potential as an offline storage area. Inverleith Park was deemed unsuitable for storage due to the topography of the area. Flood management schemes at Murrayfield are already improving the location. Murray Burn has frequent flooding and, due to the site's location in the greenbelt, it has the space required to implement a variety of methods.

Managment and Pressures

Murray Burn

In its current state, the study area could hold aproximately 188.323 m3 of water - equivalent to 69 olympic size swimming pools. To improve the current land for flood management and habitat, wads, brushes, willows and other trees would be planted along the riverbanks. A leaky barrier could be introduced to slow the river, creating a pond.
We would build paths, benches, information points and four entrance points to maximise public accessibility and usability of the green space.
