Flood Management along the Water of Leith

Flood Risk in Edinburgh

In April 2000 the traditional river management systems in place were tested and provided little protection against the flood.
To improve and prepare for an increase in flooding due to climate change, the city of Edinburgh implemented the Flood Risk Management Act and flood prevention schemes, for which walls, gates and other flood defences were built. The Harlaw, Threipmuir and Harperring reservoirs were also purchased to improve their management.

Despite this

  • 85% of the city are in need of protection
  • over 284,000 homes, businesses and services at risk
  • cost: £31.5 million per year

Factors amplefying the risk:

  • inadequate drainage systems
  • poor building construction techniques
  • outdated flood defences
  • homes & businesses in flood zones

Water of Leith catchment

Over the past 200 years the Water of Leith has been modified through dams, weirs, lochs, and culverts to accommodate economic activities and continuous urban development.

Many of the weirs and dams were built for the many mills that stood once along the river, but do not serve a purpose anymore. However due to their presence, along with invasive species, and sewage and farming runoff along the Murray Burn, the catchment is of poor ecological status.

Despite the continuous riparian woodland along the main stream, increased use of the Water of Leith Walk and new developments the corridor has been fragmentated and the quaility deteriorated. They also create more impermeable surfaces, which reduce water infiltration and increase surface runoff.

Options for changing the current managment

The Water of Leith management plan was set to address some of the issues but only focuses on the popular Water of Leith Walk. The natural flow of this section was mostly maintained due to its hard rock river bed and has seen an increase in conservation efforts through numerous action plans.

The Murray Burn on the other hand faces higher pressures with pollution and the culvert but has mostly been neglected by action plans and studies due to its comparatively smaller size.

Based on SEPA Flood, Population at Risk and River Management data: Murray Burn, Saughton Park, Murrayfield and Inverleith Park were considered flood management opportunities.

Natural river managements options:

  • offline storage areas
  • floodplain and floodplain woodlands

Murray Burn has frequent flooding and, due to the site's location in the greenbelt, it has the space required to implement a variety of methods.

Managment and Pressures


Due to the large amount of space needed, Saughton Park only has limited potential as an offline storage area. Inverleith Park was deemed unsuitable for storage due to the topography of the area. Flood management schemes at Murrayfield are already improving the location.

Murray Burn

In its current state, the study area could hold aproximately 188.323 m3 of water - equivalent to 68 olympic size swimming pools. To improve the current land for flood management and habitat, wads, brushes, willows and other trees would be planted along the riverbanks. A leaky barrier could be introduced to slow the river, creating a pond.
We would build paths, benches, information points and four entrance points to maximise public accessibility and usability of the green space.
